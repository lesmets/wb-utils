Microphone not working? Work through this

If the meter above is not moving when you speak, it is almost always one of five things, in this order:

  1. Permission. If you dismissed or blocked the prompt, the browser will not ask again. Click the lock or camera icon in the address bar, set the microphone to Allow, and reload the page.
  2. The wrong device. Use the Microphone dropdown above. Laptops with a webcam, a headset, and a built-in mic often default to whichever was plugged in last. A headset can appear twice, once for each mode.
  3. Muted hardware. Check the physical mute switch on the microphone, the inline button on a headset cable, and the mute key on your keyboard. This is the single most common cause, and the most embarrassing.
  4. Another app has it. A video call, recorder, or streaming tool may be holding the microphone exclusively. Quit it fully, not just the window, and try again.
  5. System settings. Open your operating system's sound input settings and confirm the device is enabled and its input volume is not at zero. If the meter moves there but not here, it is a browser permission problem, not a hardware one.

What a good microphone level looks like

Getting the level right matters more than the price of the microphone. Speaking normally, aim for the meter to sit in the good band, touching loud only on your most emphatic moments.

Three fixes that beat buying a new mic

Get closer. Halving the distance to the microphone is a bigger improvement than most hardware upgrades, because it raises your voice relative to the room. A hand's width away, slightly off to the side, is ideal for most headsets and desk mics.

Fix the room, not the gear. Echo comes from bare walls and hard surfaces. Soft furnishings, a rug, or curtains do more for clarity than a better capsule ever will.

Set the gain, don't shout. Adjust the input level in your operating system's sound settings until normal speech lands in the good band. Raising your voice to compensate for a low gain just makes you sound tense.

Frequently asked questions

How do I test my microphone?

Click Start microphone and allow access when your browser asks. Speak normally: the level meter should move with your voice and the waveform should ripple. If you want to hear yourself, record a few seconds and play it back. Nothing is uploaded at any point. The recording exists only in your browser's memory until you leave the page.

Is my recording uploaded or stored anywhere?

No. Audio is captured with the Web Audio API and stays in memory on your device. There is no server involved, nothing is written to disk unless you choose to download it, and closing the tab discards everything. That is a meaningful difference from mic testers that upload your recording to check it.

Why is my microphone not working?

Work through it in order. First, permission: if you blocked access, click the camera or lock icon in the address bar and allow the microphone, then reload. Second, the device: use the dropdown here to pick the right microphone, since laptops often default to the wrong one and headsets can appear twice. Third, mute: check the physical switch on the mic, headset, or laptop function key. Fourth, another app: a video call already using the mic can hold it exclusively, so quit it and try again. Finally, check your operating system's own sound input settings.

What is a good microphone level?

When you speak at a normal volume, the meter should sit roughly between a third and three quarters of the way across, peaking near the top only on your loudest moments. Too quiet and listeners strain to hear you while background hiss becomes obvious. Too loud and the signal clips, which sounds harsh and crackly and cannot be repaired afterwards. Adjust the input gain in your operating system's sound settings rather than by shouting or backing away.

Does this test work on a phone?

Yes. It works in any modern mobile browser and will use your phone's built-in microphone, or a connected headset if you pick it in the device list. Note that browsers require a secure connection for microphone access, which this site uses. Some mobile browsers also stop the audio when the tab moves to the background, so keep the page in view while testing.

Why does my voice sound different when I play it back?

That is normal and not a fault with your microphone. When you speak, you hear yourself partly through the bones of your skull, which carries lower frequencies that never reach a microphone. Recorded playback is only the airborne sound, so it seems thinner and higher pitched than the voice in your head. It is what everyone else has always heard.
